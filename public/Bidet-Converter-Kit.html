The Three (and a half) Types of Bidet Converter Kit

The Pros and Cons of Different Bidet Converter Kits


Attachments

Let's start with the most basic type of bidet converter kit - the attachment. To add an attachment, you remove your toilet seat, pop on the attachment, and put your toilet seat back on. They're pretty straightforward, it's like a little water fountain you add to your toilet.


They're pretty cheap and convenient, but be careful, you might get something so cheap it's missing key features that could be a real pain in the butt. Pro tip: don't cheap out when it comes to warm water, your butt will thank you later.


Possible Features:

  • Front nozzle
  • Customizable stray strength
  • Self-cleaning
  • Retractable Nozzle
  • Warm Water


Pros:

  • The attachment changes the least about your toilet seat, so if you're scared of change it's a great intro. (It's also the easiest to install)
  • Generally speaking these are the least expensive options, sometimes as little as $30 (although don't be afraid to spoil your butt, you won't regret it).
  • More ability to pick and choose individual features you want instead of just paying for a ton of things at once.
  • Easy to attach/detach, so you can take them with you between houses if you move.


Cons:

  • Pretty limited in functionality. Most attachments can only spray cold water, sometimes with varying strength.
  • More prone to splashing.
  • You may need to purchase spacers to put in between the toilet seat and the rim to make the seat level.


Non-Electric Bidet Seat

It's like an attachment, but you don't have to worry about fitting it into your current toilet seat! Instead, it becomes your toilet seat! Sometimes with attachments you have to buy spacers and other things to rig up the setup to work well, but with a seat it's generally all built-in and just easier overall.


Installation is a bit more complex, you'll want to see if your bathroom is well setup for this type of seat (really depends on where your sink is), but this is a great middle-of-the-road option for people looking for something solid but not too expensive.


Possible Features:

  • Front Nozzle
  • Customizable spray strength
  • Retractable nozzle
  • Self cleaning
  • Warm water


Pros:

  • Much cheaper than electric models
  • Stronger water flow than attachments
  • Less prone to splashing
  • No need for spacers to make the toilet seat level
  • More control than an attachment (may have dials/buttons)


Cons:

  • Need a more specific bathroom setup. The closer your sink and toilet are, the better.
  • Most often have cold water
  • Way fewer features than electric


Handheld

Imagine a pressure washer for your butt. That's handheld baby! Lovingly known as the "bum gun", it makes cleaning your butt a blast. It seems really weird at first, I know, but trust me within a week you'll be ass-tronomically impressed.


Although there are fewer features, the ability to aim the stream is actually really important and can help you get way more clean than options like attachments that might not have what I call "Full Crack Coverage" (The water doesn't move, so you have to.)


So if you want a cheap option and you're willing to put a little holster on the wall/toilet, I 100% recommend handhelds.


Possible Features:

  • Customizable spray strength (normally a handle you can squeeze harder)
  • Full Crack Coverage
  • Warm water (be careful if you decide to attach them to your hot water pipe)


Pros:

  • No need for spacers or adjusting your current toilet seat
  • Affordable
  • Full Crack Coverage at a great price


Cons:

  • Water flow can be too strong sometimes (be sure to get one with a handle if you can)
  • Most often cold water (can be hell in the winter depending on your pipes)
  • Need to aim (you won't be a sharpshooter on day one)
  • Your hand will get a little wet
